Farming for the Future: Power, Ethics, challenges of AI in Agriculture

Rebal Mallick Clock Thurs 01, 2023 3

>>> Artificial Intelligence (AI) has made some incredible strides lately with its mass popularity growing due to ChatGPT by Open AI. This has caused many industries to revaluate the possibilities of AI and look further into integration. That’s right ‘further' as in have already been looking at AI integration, an example of such an industry is Agriculture. The agriculture industry is the frontrunner when it comes to embracing advancing technology. They do this to ensure efficient operation, productive output of crops and build a sustainable practice for future generations. Yet, these goals are becoming harder to achieve for the current agricultural production system due to “population growth, shrinking farmland, dwindling natural resources, erratic climate changes, and shifting market demands”. For instance, according to the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations by 2050 the world’s population will reach over 9 billion which creates an urgency to develop the agriculture industry (Liu Simon). The integration of Artificial intelligence (AI) in agriculture holds promise in addressing these challenges by analyzing data and making predictions of future conditions to help eliminate issues brought about by pest infection, low crop yield, droughts, and climate change. However, it is crucial to examine the ethical and social implications of AI in Agriculture instead of jumping on the AI trend due to ChatGPT. You see unlike ethical issues of students using ChatGPT for cheating, the implications of AI in Agriculture are much greater as it influences food and farmers, which are vital for the food we consume daily. In this blog post, with my background in Computer Science and with the help of academic sources will explore how AI in Agriculture interacts with ethical and social issues and why it is essential to address classification, fairness, and job displacement in the context of AI as it continues to grow.

The Power of AI in Agriculture:

Artificial intelligence (AI) makes use of machine learning algorithms such as neural networks, and decision trees to compile data and make predictions of future conditions. This is facilitated by a large amount of data about crops and soil conditions continuously gathered by the advancement in computers, the use of satellite imagery, and remote sensing technology since the 1990s. This large collection of data allowed for the development of machine learning algorithms designed for agriculture leading to AI in Agriculture. The power of AI in agriculture can be seen in the Indian state of Andhra Pradesh, where Microsoft Corporation is working with farmers and providing advisory services through AI-based sowing applications. This AI application recommends sowing data, methods of cultivable land preparation, seed treatment, soil analysis and more to suggest optimization for farming crops. Through this understanding provided farmers found a 30% increase in average crop yield per ha (2.471 acres). The AI-based sowing approach is based on data recognizing optimal sowing performance in various seasons and climatic conditions using Moisture Adequacy Data(MAI) (Dharmaraj, V. and Vijayanand, C). Although AI adoption in Agriculture presents opportunities in agriculture, there are some challenges and issues that slow its process. Such as the large amount of data required for training machines to take precise forecasting. However, this issue is one that the agriculture industry is continuing to improve daily. Therefore, it is important to take this time to look at the ethical implications of AI in Agriculture.

Ethical and Social Issues in AI Agriculture:

Classification and fairness are crucial to consider as AI is intergraded into agriculture as it can cause job displacement for farmers. Classification forms the foundation of AI systems in agriculture as it enables the application to provide recommendations for planting, pest management, crop health and more. It is the process where data is put through a process of identifying patterns and categorizing this information to build the model for the AI algorithm. Yet, this process of identifying data can introduce errors and biases into classification due to choices made regarding the training data, attributes used by the algorithm, and a class of definitions. It is vital to ensure that data used to train the AI model comes from a diverse range of farmers and crops instead of just Western origins to prevent discrimination and promote fairness in agriculture. You see the lack of diversity in data can cause the AI to make biased decisions that favor larger agricultural enterprises putting smaller-scale farmers at a disadvantage. Or it can cause the issue of discrimination against other agricultural practices due lack of data to be trained on during the classification process resulting in the AI forming a skewed stance. For example, the three-sister garden practice used by the indigenous community, which uses the concept of relationship shared by corn, squash, and beans to maximize the growth of crops (Cornell University) may be disregarded by the AI. If not handled correctly this can lead to the ethical and social issue of job displacement and de-skilling workers.

Lessons from Similar Contexts:

To gain an understanding of the impact of biases and job displacement issues, we can draw lessons from other domains. For instance, the COMPAS algorithm, in the criminal justice system, which aimed to predict recidivism risk for judges, was found to exhibit biases against individuals of colour. Black defendants were predicted to re-offend 45 percent more than their white counterparts of 23 percent (Jeff Larson, Surya Mattu, Lauren Kirchner, and Julia Angwin). This bias stemmed from the input data used for training the COMPAS classification process, highlighting the need for unbiased data and diverse stakeholder involvement in AI Agriculture systems. Another case that illustrates the impact of the advancement of technology on job displacement is Expert system automation. This case of automation resulted in de-skilling and alienating workers during the industrial revolution. The introduction of these automation systems where workers have no control caused workers to feel “disembodied and reembodied as machines”(Tavani, pg. 282-3) and resulted in a disconnect in the relationship between workers and the workplace. This is why it is important to include farmers during the development of AI in agriculture. These cases demonstrate the need to consider any biases in the data set and avoid them as it can result in a comparable consequence on farmers as well as involving farmers in the process to insure fairness.

Addressing the Ethical Challenges:

We must take proactive steps to overcome the ethical and social challenges brought by AI in agriculture. A need for diverse stakeholders to participate in this process is required to develop a fair AI system that ensures an equal relationship with farms rather than replacing farmers. This entails that we include small-scale farmers, large-scale farmers, different agricultural origins, and even consumers. By taking this diverse pool of stakeholders we can ensure that the training data for the AI is fair and unbiased as it meets the needs of all stakeholders. This also means that we must upskill the farmers in the AI system to ensure that they are able to understand the decisions made by the AI system. This allows for job security and enables trust and fairness for Farmers allowing for the acceptance of artificial intelligence in agriculture.

AI's potential to evolve the industry is massive as it allows the optimization of crops and ensures informed decisions. This means we must not overlook the ethical and social implications of AI applications in agriculture. We can utilize the power of AI while protecting the interest of farmers and society by managing classification biases through diverse stakeholders. So as AI advances, let us strive for a more diverse and fair practice in agriculture. <<<
